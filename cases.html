Cases and Form Factors

Motherboard form factor standards help ensure that computer components can be interchangeable among vendors and generations of technology. Knowing form factors is important when customizing and upgrading systems.

Placeholder for Image

What is a Form Factor?

A form factor determines the physical characteristics of a motherboard, including:

  • Dimensions
  • Power supply type
  • Mounting hole locations
  • Number of expansion slots
  • Back panel arrangement

Common Motherboard Form Factors

Form Factor
Characteristics
ATX
Most used form factor, measures 12" × 9.6", up to 7 expansion slots
microATX
Smaller version of ATX, 9.6" × 9.6", 4 expansion slots
Mini-ITX
Designed for small form factor PCs, 6.7" × 6.7", 1 expansion slot

Common Computer Cases

  • ATX Full-Tower: Largest case type, supports Standard ATX, EATX, and microATX.
  • ATX Mid-Tower: Slightly smaller than full-tower, supports Standard ATX, microATX, and Mini-ITX.
  • microATX Tower: Compact, designed for desktops, typically supports microATX and Mini-ITX.
  • Mini-ITX Tower: Smallest standard case type, supports Mini-ITX motherboards.

Troubleshooting Tips

  • Ensure your case supports your motherboard's form factor.
  • Check that the power supply provides enough wattage for all components.
  • Verify proper airflow and install case fans if necessary.
  • Use standoffs when mounting the motherboard to prevent short circuits.
